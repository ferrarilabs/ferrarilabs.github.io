About

Some background on how I got here.

I started in financial crime compliance before most institutions had dedicated crypto programs, before model risk governance was a standard exam topic, and before BSA officers had to explain AI to their boards. A lot has changed. The underlying problems have not.

Most of my work has been inside the details: reviewing segmentation logic with data teams, sitting in case review sessions with investigators, challenging model documentation with validators, preparing materials that need to hold up in front of an OCC or NYDFS examiner. That is where you learn what actually breaks and why.

I have worked with compliance officers, CROs, BSA teams, model risk groups, internal audit, and the technology teams building the systems underneath all of it. The cross-functional part matters. A monitoring program that makes sense to the quant team but cannot be explained by a BSA officer in an exam is a liability, not an asset.

I also build. Not because it sounds interesting — because there are specific problems in this space that tooling still handles poorly, and I would rather fix them than keep working around them.

Work

Where I spend most of my time.

01

AML Programs

Transaction monitoring assessment, alert logic review, case management workflow, SAR quality, escalation path design, and the documentation that examiners actually read. Covers both rule-based and model-driven programs.

02

Model Validation

Independent challenge of detection models, scoring logic, and threshold-setting. Explainability, backtesting, conceptual soundness documentation, and preparing for OCC 2011-12 and NYDFS exam questions that go deeper than most teams expect.

03

AI in Compliance

Where AI adds real value in financial crime detection, where it creates governance problems, and how to design systems that can be explained to an auditor and defended to a regulator — not just benchmarked on internal metrics.

04

Digital Asset Risk

Compliance architecture for crypto-native institutions and banks with indirect exposure. Blockchain analytics integration, Travel Rule and TRISA implementation, VASP counterparty risk, and sanctions screening for on-chain activity.

05

Sanctions & Screening

Watch list coverage, name matching configuration, false positive management, correspondent banking risk, and controls for cross-border and digital asset payment flows. OFAC SDN, UN, EU, and OFSI list management.

06

KYC / CDD / EDD

Onboarding controls, customer risk scoring methodology, beneficial ownership gaps, enhanced due diligence workflows, and the data infrastructure that determines whether any of it is actually usable when a case opens.

From the field

Things I keep writing about because they keep coming up.

Your bank does not touch crypto. That does not mean crypto is not touching your bank.

Crypto exposure arrives through customer activity, payment behavior, and correspondent relationships — often well before any deliberate decision to enter the space.

LinkedIn →

Model validation is where compliance programs tend to be most exposed.

Not because the models are wrong. Because institutions cannot explain the decisions, reproduce the outputs, or show what changed between versions when an examiner asks.

LinkedIn →

Deploying AI in financial crime is not primarily a technology decision.

The harder questions are governance: who signs off on a threshold change, how do you document it, and what happens when an investigator disagrees with a model score.
